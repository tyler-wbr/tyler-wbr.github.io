Carolina Panthers Salary Distribution Analysis

Introduction

To prevent large-market, high-valued NFL teams from acquiring the best players by offering them the highest salaries, each team must operate under a strict salary cap. This is the maximum limit of money each NFL team can spend on its players, determined by the NFL every year in order to promote a competitive balance throughout the league. The problem this project addresses is understanding how the Carolina Panthers distribute their salary cap spending in comparison to the rest of the league.

Key questions include:

  • Which positions are the Panthers investing in most heavily?
  • Are spending priorities aligned with league trends?
  • Does their distribution suggest strengths or weaknesses in roster construction?

The Data

My data comes from OverTheCap, accessed through the nflreadr R package. It contains contract details for every NFL player, both active and inactive. There are 25 columns containing different attributes, but we will primarily utilize the player names, position, team, and APY (average per year salary).

Pre-processing the Data

To prepare the data for visualization, we must first apply some filters and create two separate databases to compare.

  1. Filter inactive contracts -> ensures only active contracts are analyzed

  1. Create a database for only Panthers players
  2. Group salaries by position and team → total APY spending calculated.


  1. Assign unique colors to each position -> Allows for consistency in charts.

We now have two separate databases, one for the NFL as a whole and the other for only the Panthers. These databases sum APY at each position.  They both only contain active contracts.

Data Understanding & Visualization

To explore the data, I created:

  • Grouped bar chart comparing average salary by position for the Panthers vs. league average
  • Pie chart to see the percentage of total spending across positions.

First impressions/Findings:

Center (C) anomaly

  • Data shows $0 allocated to center.
  • Reality: starting center Austin Corbett is listed as RG, inflating guard spending.

Interior Defensive Line (IDL) over-investment

  • Panthers’ IDL spending is nearly double the league average.
  • Pushed by Derrick Brown’s $24M APY contract (4th highest at his position) and additional contracts (Wharton, Brown III, Robinson) that amount to an additional $29M.

Linebacker (LB) under-investment

  • LB spending is well below league average.
  • Matches on-field struggles as the Panthers ranked last in run defense last season.

Quarterback (QB) low spending

  • QB spending is significantly lower than league average.
  • Explained by Bryce Young’s rookie contract (cheap relative to veteran QBs).

Wide Receiver (WR) shortage

  • Panthers spend far below league average at WR.
  • Mirrors weak production at the position.
  • While rookie 8th overall pick  Tetairoa McMillan offers potential, the roster lacks a proven WR1.

Impact

This data clearly displays the discrepancies in how the Panthers are allocating their money. It shows potential roster balancing issues, such as a concentration on IDL and neglect of LB. It also shows comparisons to league averages, potentially showing what they should be putting money towards. Telling a story on this data is also limited in some ways. The amount of money allocated towards a position certainly doesn’t correlate to performance at that position. For example, a young star player may be one of the best at his position, but he may still be on a rookie contract, meaning he isn’t being paid like a top player at his position.
